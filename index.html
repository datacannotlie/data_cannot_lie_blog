The Impact of Federal Investment in Public Health on Cardiovascular Disease Incidence: A Comparative Analysis (2000-2020)

Published on [Date]

I have always believed that government spending on education and healthcare should be seen not as expenses, but as investments. Investing in healthcare, for example, can improve the overall health of the population, enhance quality of life, and strengthen the national workforce. This, in turn, boosts productivity and reduces early retirements and work absences due to health issues, lessening the strain on social security. Let's consider the case of cardiovascular diseases. This essay examines the relationship between federal investment in public health and the incidence of cardiovascular diseases across six countries — the United States, Brazil, Japan, Australia, Cuba, and Finland — focusing on their healthcare investments from 2000 to 2020.

Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) remain a leading cause of mortality worldwide, prompting nations to invest heavily in public health initiatives aimed at prevention and treatment ( Benjamin et al., 2019). Figure 1 illustrates the evolution of CVD rates in five countries between 2000 and 2020. Despite a reduction in CVD mortality across all the countries analyzed, it remains higher in Brazil and Cuba and lower in Japan and Australia.

When we consider the healthcare expenditure (as percentage of GDP), we observe that along the time, the investiment is increasing for all countries analyzed but USA has the highest healthcare expenditure, while Finland and Brazil present the lowest values.

A comparative analysis, showed in figure 3, reveals diverse outcomes among the six countries, highlighting the complex relationship between healthcare spending and CVD incidence. Despite high healthcare expenditures, the United States has shown only a modest decrease in CVD mortality rates (Frieden & Berwick, 2011), suggesting that high spending alone does not guarantee optimal outcomes. In contrast, Brazil's focus on preventive care and health education has led to a notable reduction in CVD mortality rates, emphasizing the importance of efficient resource allocation (Ribeiro et al., 2016). This demonstrates that middle-income nations can achieve significant improvements through targeted public health strategies. Japan's consistently low CVD incidence, attributed to a combination of factors including a healthy diet, active lifestyle, and emphasis on preventive care (Iso, 2011), further underscores the importance of cultural factors and lifestyle in CVD prevention.

The experiences of Australia, Cuba, and Finland provide additional insights into effective strategies for reducing CVD incidence. Australia has demonstrated a strong correlation between increased healthcare spending and reduced CVD mortality, highlighting the importance of well-targeted public health investments (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, 2021). Cuba's remarkable success in reducing CVD mortality rates, despite limited economic resources, shows that efficient allocation of resources can be crucial (Cooper et al., 2006). This underscores the potential for significant health improvements even with constrained budgets. Finland's comprehensive approach, including the North Karelia Project, serves as a model for other countries. It emphasizes the importance of long-term commitment to public health initiatives and addressing social determinants of health, such as education, housing, and employment (Puska, 2002).

Across all studied countries, the role of technology in CVD prevention and treatment has become increasingly important. Investments in digital health infrastructure, telemedicine, and mobile health applications have improved access to healthcare services, particularly in rural and underserved areas (World Health Organization, 2019). Additionally, research in genomics and precision medicine has led to targeted interventions and personalized treatment plans, enhancing the effectiveness of CVD prevention and treatment (National Institutes of Health, 2020). Community-based initiatives, such as public health campaigns and health education programs, have also been effective in promoting healthy lifestyles and reducing CVD risk factors (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2020).

Figure 3: Cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality rate per 100,000 people from 2000 to 2020 as function of the healthcare expenditure for Australia, Cuba, Japan, Brazil, Finland, and the United States, for the period between 2000 and 2020.

The evidence suggests that increased investment in public health is generally associated with better outcomes, but the effectiveness of this spending varies significantly based on how it is allocated and the specific context of each country. Key factors that enhance the impact of healthcare expenditures include a focus on preventive care, public health education, policies promoting healthy lifestyles, equitable access to healthcare services, integration of CVD prevention into primary care, and investment in research and innovation.

Figure 4: Projeted healthcare expenditure needed to minimize CVD deaths in Australia, Cuba, Japan, Brazil, Finland, and the United States.

Figure 4 presents a theoretical value for healthcare investments needed to minimize CVD incidence in the countries considered here. This value is derived from the projection of the linear regression between CVD incidence and healthcare expenditure. While this is a theoretical value and CVD is complex, it provides a reasonable estimate. As observed in Figure 3, CVD mortality rates in Finland, Australia, and Brazil are more sensitive to healthcare expenditure. Therefore, Figure 4 shows that these three nations could achieve minimized levels of CVD mortality with relatively lower healthcare investments. This would represent a little more than a 50% increase in healthcare investment for Brazil and even less for Finland and Australia. However, for Cuba, minimizing the CVD death rate would require an investment of 45% of GDP, which may be impractical while the economic blockade persists.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the comparative analysis reveals that while increased healthcare expenditure is associated with reduced CVD incidence, the relationship is complex and influenced by multiple factors. To maximize the impact of federal investments in public health on CVD incidence, policymakers should focus on evidence-based interventions, preventive care, and addressing social determinants of health. The experiences of these nations underscore the importance of sustained, strategic investment in public health to combat cardiovascular diseases effectively. As global health challenges continue to evolve, countries must adapt their strategies, learn from successful models, and prioritize efficient resource allocation to effectively reduce the burden of cardiovascular diseases and improve overall public health outcomes.
